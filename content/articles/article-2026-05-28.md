# PSLF Is Changing July 1, 2026: What Doctors Need to Know Before Buying a Home

If you're a physician working at a nonprofit hospital, academic medical center, or government clinic — and you've been counting on Public Service Loan Forgiveness to handle your student debt — the next few weeks matter more than you might think.

Starting July 1, 2026, the Department of Education gains new authority to disqualify certain employers from PSLF eligibility. For doctors who've spent years building toward the 10-year forgiveness finish line, this introduces meaningful uncertainty into a plan that used to feel rock-solid.

And here's why that matters for home buyers specifically: your student loan repayment situation is one of the most important factors in whether — and how much home — you can afford to buy.

## What's Actually Changing

The July 1 changes do two things that doctors should understand:

**First**, the Department of Education now has expanded authority to determine whether an employer qualifies for PSLF. What used to be a relatively straightforward determination based on your employer's nonprofit or government status is becoming more variable. If your hospital or health system gets reclassified, years of qualifying payments could be affected.

**Second**, changes to income-driven repayment (IDR) plans may cause monthly payment amounts to shift upward for some borrowers. This is significant because your IDR payment is the figure most physician mortgage lenders use when calculating your debt-to-income (DTI) ratio.

## Why This Affects Your Mortgage

Here's the connection most doctors miss: your mortgage qualification is built around your monthly obligations, not your total debt balance. Physician mortgage programs — the specialized home loans designed for doctors — typically use your actual IDR payment in the DTI calculation rather than a percentage of your total loan balance. That's a huge advantage over conventional loans, which often impute a much higher payment.

But if your IDR payment goes up because of plan restructuring, your DTI ratio goes up too. A higher DTI can reduce the loan amount you qualify for, push you into a higher rate tier, or in some cases cause you to fall outside a lender's guidelines entirely.

The math matters. Say your income-driven payment is currently $200/month, but plan changes push it to $600/month. On a physician mortgage application, that $400 difference in monthly obligations could translate to a meaningfully lower qualifying loan amount — sometimes by $60,000–$100,000 or more, depending on the lender.

## What About PSLF as a Home-Buying Strategy?

Some physicians have deliberately delayed buying a home because they're counting on PSLF to eliminate their debt in the future. The logic: why tie up cash in a down payment or take on a mortgage while you're still carrying $200,000+ in student loans? Wait for forgiveness, then buy.

That strategy made sense when PSLF was predictable. With July 1 introducing new uncertainty, some doctors may want to revisit the assumption. A few questions worth thinking through:

- **Is your employer at risk?** If you work at a large academic medical center or well-established nonprofit, your risk is likely low. If you work at a smaller or more administratively complex institution, it may be worth verifying your employer's status before July.
- **How many years of payments do you have left?** If you're 7–9 years in, staying the course probably still makes sense regardless of the changes. If you're 1–3 years in, the new uncertainty may change your calculus.
- **What would your monthly payment look like under the revised plans?** A student loan advisor or financial planner can model this for your specific loans and income.

## The 2026 Housing Market Angle

Here's the timing consideration that makes this especially interesting: 2026 is shaping up to be one of the better windows for home buyers in recent memory. Income growth is expected to outpace home price growth for the first time since the post-Great Recession era. Mortgage rates are projected to remain in the low-to-mid 6% range. Inventory has improved in many markets.

For high-income earners like physicians — who can access physician mortgage programs with 0% down and no private mortgage insurance — the affordability calculus looks better this year than it has in a while. If PSLF uncertainty is causing you to reassess a "wait for forgiveness" approach, the external conditions are relatively favorable for making a move.

That doesn't mean rushing into a purchase without a plan. It means that the conversation is worth having now, before July 1, while you still have full clarity on your current repayment obligations.

## Practical Steps Before July 1

If you're a physician considering a home purchase in the next 6–18 months:

**Review your PSLF employer certification.** Make sure your current employer is on record as qualifying, and check whether there's any administrative uncertainty about your institution's status.

**Know your current IDR payment — in writing.** Pull your loan servicer records and confirm exactly what your monthly payment is under your current plan. This is the number that matters to a physician mortgage lender.

**Model the "what if" scenarios.** Ask your student loan servicer or a financial advisor what your payment would look like under plan alternatives. Run the DTI math with a physician mortgage lender before you fall in love with a house.

**Don't assume you need to wait.** Many physicians are surprised to learn they can qualify for a physician mortgage even with $200,000+ in student loans — because of how these programs treat debt. Getting pre-qualified costs you nothing and gives you much more information than guessing.
