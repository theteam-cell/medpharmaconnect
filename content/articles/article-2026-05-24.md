# The Student Loan Cliff: How New 2026 Federal Rules Could Affect Your Path to Homeownership

If you're a medical professional planning to buy a home in the next few years, there's a policy change you need to understand before July 1, 2026. New federal rules are reshaping the student loan landscape in ways that could directly affect how lenders evaluate your mortgage application.

Here's what's changing, why it matters for physicians, dentists, and pharmacists, and what you can do about it.

## What's Changing on July 1, 2026

Congress has enacted significant reforms to federal graduate lending, and the consequences for future medical borrowers are substantial.

**Borrowing limits are dropping.** Direct Unsubsidized Loans will be capped at $50,000 per year and $200,000 total for graduate and professional students. The Grad PLUS loan program — which many medical students have relied on to bridge the gap between standard loan limits and their actual tuition bills — will be eliminated for new borrowers entirely.

**Repayment options are being consolidated.** New borrowers will have access to just two repayment plans: a Tiered Standard Plan and the new Repayment Assistance Plan (RAP). RAP will replace all existing income-driven repayment (IDR) options, including PAYE, SAVE, and IBR.

**PSLF eligibility is narrowing.** For new borrowers who didn't have eligible loans before June 30, 2025, medical residency training programs will no longer count as qualifying employment under Public Service Loan Forgiveness. Only RAP-enrolled borrowers will be eligible for PSLF going forward.

## Why This Matters for Your Mortgage

You might be wondering: I'm already through training, or I borrowed before the deadline — why does this affect me? The answer is that these changes reshape the entire ecosystem that physician mortgage lenders operate within.

**The DTI equation is shifting.** Debt-to-income ratio is one of the most important factors in mortgage underwriting. Under the old system, doctors on income-driven repayment plans sometimes had very low monthly payment figures — which lenders used favorably when calculating DTI. Under RAP, payment calculations work differently, and in some scenarios may show a higher required monthly payment on paper, which could affect the loan amounts doctors qualify for.

**Future colleagues will carry more private debt.** With federal Grad PLUS eliminated and federal caps lowered, the next generation of medical students will need to turn to private lenders to cover the gap — often at higher interest rates and without income-driven repayment protections. Medical graduates entering the workforce after 2030 may arrive with a mix of federal and private debt that physician loan underwriters will need to navigate more carefully.

**PSLF strategy is changing.** For many residents considering hospital employment as a pathway to tax-free loan forgiveness, the calculus is shifting. Some physicians who had counted on 10-year PSLF forgiveness may need to reconsider their financial plan, which in turn affects how aggressively they can save for a down payment or what monthly budget they have for a mortgage.

## What You Should Do Right Now

If you're already in practice and borrowed before June 30, 2025, your existing loans are grandfathered under the old rules. But that doesn't mean you're insulated from the downstream effects. Here's how to stay ahead of the curve.

**Get clarity on your current repayment plan and how it's reported.** When you apply for a physician mortgage, lenders will ask about your monthly student loan payment. Make sure you understand what your current plan reports as your required monthly payment — and get a document from your servicer confirming it. This number directly affects your mortgage approval and loan amount.

**Work with a lender who understands medical professional lending.** Not all mortgage lenders are equipped to interpret the nuances of physician loan applications, especially with the new repayment plan landscape. Physician-focused lenders have experience understanding that a resident on RAP isn't the same credit risk as a borrower on a standard 10-year plan with the same balance.

**Don't delay unnecessarily.** If you're an attending physician who's been sitting on the fence about buying a home, the 2026 housing market is actually showing some favorable signs — easing inventory, modest rate decreases projected, and lenders competing aggressively for physician borrowers. Waiting another year or two doesn't guarantee better conditions, and the student loan policy picture is unlikely to become simpler.

**Talk to a financial advisor who specializes in physician finances.** The intersection of student loan strategy and homeownership timing is genuinely complex. Decisions about which repayment plan to use, when to refinance, and when to buy a home are interconnected in ways that general-purpose financial advice often misses.

## The Bottom Line

The July 1, 2026 student loan changes are significant — but they're not a reason to panic or to postpone your homeownership plans indefinitely. For physicians who trained under the old rules and are ready to buy, conditions are actually reasonably favorable right now. The key is understanding how your specific loan situation interacts with physician mortgage underwriting, and working with professionals who know this landscape well.
