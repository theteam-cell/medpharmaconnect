# The Student Loan Shakeup of 2026: What It Means for Your Physician Mortgage

If you've been following federal student loan news, you know 2026 is a pivotal year. Major policy changes are set to take effect on **July 1, 2026**, and for physicians, dentists, and pharmacists navigating home ownership, the implications go beyond monthly loan payments — they directly touch your ability to qualify for a mortgage.

Here's a clear breakdown of what's changing and what it means if you're planning to buy a home in the next 6–18 months.

## What's Actually Changing on July 1, 2026

### Grad PLUS Loans Are Gone for New Borrowers

The Federal Grad PLUS loan program — which allowed graduate and professional students to borrow up to the full cost of attendance — is being eliminated for new borrowers. In its place, unsubsidized Stafford loans will be capped at **$50,000 per year** with a **$200,000 lifetime maximum** for professional degree programs.

For context, the average medical school debt at graduation currently exceeds $200,000. This cap creates significant funding gaps for incoming medical and dental students who haven't yet started borrowing.

If you're already a physician with existing loans, these caps don't apply to you retroactively. But if you're a resident or fellow who entered medical school before July 1, 2026, review your status carefully — there are grandfather protections, but they come with conditions.

### PSLF Is Changing — Residency Years No Longer Count

This is the one that stings most for residents and fellows pursuing Public Service Loan Forgiveness. Under the new rules, **years spent in residency and fellowship will no longer count** toward the 120-payment threshold required for PSLF forgiveness.

Previously, a physician completing a 5-year residency at a qualifying nonprofit hospital could count all five years toward forgiveness — meaning they'd only need 5 more years as an attending to reach the 120-payment mark. Under the new framework, those residency years are wiped off the clock.

For a physician finishing a 5-year residency today, that's potentially five extra years of payments added to their forgiveness timeline.

### The New Repayment Assistance Plan (RAP)

All existing income-driven repayment plans — IBR, PAYE, REPAYE, SAVE — are being replaced for new borrowers by a single plan called the **Repayment Assistance Plan (RAP)**. RAP will have a maximum repayment term of 30 years for medical school borrowers and will be the only IDR plan eligible for PSLF going forward.

For mortgage purposes, this matters because **how your monthly payment is calculated under RAP** affects your debt-to-income (DTI) ratio — one of the most important factors lenders use when evaluating your loan application.

## How These Changes Affect Your Physician Mortgage Qualification

Physician mortgage lenders have always handled student loan debt differently than conventional lenders. Most physician loan programs use one of three methods to count your student loan debt in your DTI:

1. **The actual monthly payment** on your income-driven repayment plan
2. **A percentage of your loan balance** (typically 0.5%–1% per month)
3. **Fully amortized payment** over a standard 10-year repayment schedule

Under old IDR plans, residents with $250,000 in debt might show a monthly payment of just $200–$400, making it easy to qualify for a physician mortgage during training. Under the new RAP program, your payment calculation may differ — and lenders are still updating their underwriting policies to reflect the new plan.

**The practical takeaway:** If you're a resident or fellow planning to buy a home, ask your lender specifically how they treat RAP payments in their DTI calculations. This question matters more now than it did six months ago.

## What Doctors Should Do Before July 1

**1. If you're in training and considering a home purchase:** Move up your timeline if possible. Lock in a rate and get pre-approved before July 1. Physician mortgage lenders are aware of the policy changes and some are proactively adjusting guidelines — earlier is better.

**2. If you're planning to pursue PSLF:** Recalculate your forgiveness timeline with the new rules. The loss of residency credit may change whether PSLF still makes financial sense for your situation. A fee-only financial advisor familiar with physician finances can help you model this.

**3. If you're an incoming medical student:** Explore scholarship programs, institutional aid, and state loan repayment programs as alternatives to federal loans, given the new borrowing caps.

**4. For everyone:** Re-examine how your student loans are being handled in mortgage underwriting. The shift from SAVE/PAYE to RAP is new enough that not all lenders have updated their processes. Shop multiple physician mortgage lenders and compare DTI treatment.

## The Bigger Picture for 2026 Home Buyers

Despite the student loan turbulence, the 2026 housing market is actually showing some promising signs for high-income professionals. Mortgage rates have drifted down into the mid-6% range, home price growth has stalled near 0% nationally, and more lenders are competing for physician borrowers — which means better terms and flexibility.

The physicians, dentists, and pharmacists who navigate this moment well will be the ones who understand both sides of the equation: the new student loan landscape *and* the current mortgage market. Getting pre-approved by a lender who specializes in medical professional loans — and who understands how RAP payments work — puts you in the strongest possible position.

The rules have changed. Now it's about adapting your strategy accordingly.
