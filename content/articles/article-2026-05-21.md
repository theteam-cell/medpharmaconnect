# When the appraisal comes in low: the valuation step that a 0%-down physician mortgage can't afford to ignore

Most of what doctors, dentists, and pharmacists read about physician mortgages stops at pre-approval. Get your debt-to-income ratio sorted, shop three to five lenders, lock a rate — done. But there is a step between "offer accepted" and "keys in hand" that gets almost no airtime and can quietly unwind an otherwise clean file: the appraisal.

Here is why it matters more on a physician loan than on almost any other product, and what to do if the number comes in low.

## Why a low appraisal hits a physician loan harder

When you go under contract, your lender orders an independent appraisal to confirm the home is worth what you agreed to pay. The lender will only finance against the **lower of the contract price or the appraised value.** If you agree to $700,000 and the appraisal comes back at $675,000, the lender treats the deal as a $675,000 home. That $25,000 difference is the "appraisal gap."

On a conventional loan with 20% down, a gap is uncomfortable but absorbable — the borrower already has a cash cushion in the deal. A physician mortgage is built on the opposite premise. Its signature feature is **0% to 10% down**, which means there is little or no equity buffer to soak up a shortfall. When the appraisal comes in low, the gap does not shrink the loan you needed — it lands directly on you as cash you have to find, on top of an already-thin down payment. A buyer who chose a physician loan specifically to preserve cash can suddenly be asked to produce a five-figure sum on two weeks' notice.

That is the structural reality: the same low-down-payment benefit that makes the physician product attractive also removes the shock absorber. The appraisal is not a formality on these files. It is a real risk worth planning for.

## Where 2026 appraisal risk is concentrated

The good news is that broad appraisal-gap risk is lower today than during the 2021–2022 frenzy. With moderating price growth and far less bidding-war pressure, fewer buyers are signing contracts well above list, so fewer deals are set up to miss. But 2026 has its own quieter version of the problem, and it is concentrated rather than universal.

In a balanced, stabilizing market, appraisers tend to turn **more conservative**, leaning heavily on historical sales data that may not capture current demand or a home's unique upgrades. Years of elevated rates also thinned out transaction volume, so there are simply **fewer recent comparable sales** for an appraiser to work from — less data, less precise valuations, higher odds of a miss. Risk is also intensely **local**: newer-construction markets that boomed a few years ago are cooling while others firm up, so two metros an hour apart can carry very different appraisal odds. And in areas where insurers have pulled back, dwindling sales leave appraisers reaching for older, outdated comps.

The practical read: ask your agent directly how comp depth looks in the specific neighborhood and price band you are shopping. A custom or recently renovated home in a thin-sales pocket is where physician buyers should plan most carefully.

## A five-step playbook if the number comes in low

If the appraisal lands under contract, you usually have more room to maneuver than the first panic suggests.

**One: keep an appraisal contingency in your offer.** In a balanced 2026 market you rarely need to waive it. The contingency is your right to renegotiate or walk without losing your earnest money.

**Two: renegotiate with the seller.** A low appraisal is independent third-party evidence. Many sellers will meet you partway — or fully — rather than relist and risk the next buyer's appraiser reaching the same number.

**Three: request a reconsideration of value.** If you and your agent can document better comparable sales the appraiser overlooked, or factual errors in the report, the lender can submit a formal reconsideration. It does not always work, but it is low-cost to try.

**Four: split the difference, or cover the gap.** If the home is worth it to you, you can bring extra cash to closing to bridge the gap. Know that number *before* you waive anything — and ask your loan officer how it interacts with your down-payment tier.

**Five: walk.** With your contingency intact, you can exit. In a market with rising inventory, the next house is a real option, not a consolation prize.

## The bottom line

The physician mortgage's low down payment is a genuine advantage — right up until a low appraisal turns it into exposure. Budget a realistic cash reserve beyond your down payment, keep your appraisal contingency, and ask early about comp depth in your target neighborhood. The appraisal is the one underwriting step you cannot fully control, so the move is to plan for it rather than be surprised by it.
