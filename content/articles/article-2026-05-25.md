# The Student Loan Deadline Every Physician Home Buyer Should Know About

If you're a physician with federal student loans — or you're still in residency — there's a date on the calendar that deserves your full attention: **July 1, 2026**.

On that date, sweeping changes to federal student loan programs take effect. Some of these changes will alter the financial calculus of home buying for doctors, residents, and fellows in meaningful ways. This isn't a hypothetical. The rules are changing, the clock is ticking, and understanding the landscape now gives you more options than you'll have later.

---

## What's Changing on July 1, 2026?

### 1. Residency May No Longer Count Toward PSLF

Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF) has long been a significant part of the financial planning picture for physicians at nonprofit hospitals and academic medical centers. The program forgives remaining loan balances after 120 qualifying payments while working for a qualifying employer.

Under the new rules, **medical residencies will no longer count as qualifying public service employment** for borrowers who did not take out a Direct PLUS Loan or Direct Unsubsidized Loan before June 30, 2025.

What does that mean in practice? If you're a physician finishing a five-year residency after this cutoff, you lose five years of PSLF credit that previous generations of doctors received. For someone counting on forgiveness after 10 years of qualifying payments, that's a substantial setback — it effectively moves the forgiveness horizon years further into your attending career.

### 2. Grad PLUS Loans Are Being Eliminated

The Federal Grad PLUS Loan program — which many medical students have used to cover the gap between cost of attendance and other federal loan limits — **will no longer be available to new borrowers** after July 1, 2026. Medical school is expensive. Losing Grad PLUS access could push future students toward private loans, which typically carry less favorable repayment flexibility.

### 3. Annual and Lifetime Borrowing Caps

New federal borrowers will face a cap of **$50,000 per year and $200,000 lifetime** on Direct Unsubsidized Loans. Given that medical school can cost well over $300,000 in total, this represents a significant tightening of federal loan access.

### 4. Repayment Plans Consolidate

New borrowers will be limited to two repayment plans: a **Tiered Standard Plan** and a **Repayment Assistance Plan (RAP)**. RAP will be the only income-driven option eligible for PSLF going forward. Understanding how your payment under the new plans affects your debt-to-income ratio is increasingly important as you plan a home purchase.

## What This Means If You're Thinking About Buying a Home

The student loan policy shift creates a few distinct situations depending on where you are in your training.

**If you're a current resident or fellow:** Your existing loans are largely unaffected by the July 1 rule changes if you borrowed before the cutoff. However, if you're counting on PSLF, revisit whether your residency program and future employer qualify. The program still works — but the details matter more now.

**If you're an attending starting to think about buying:** The new repayment landscape may actually clarify your financial picture. With repayment plans simplified, your monthly student loan payment may be more predictable — which makes it easier to model your home-buying budget.

**If your PSLF timeline has shifted:** Some physicians who expected forgiveness to arrive sooner are now recalibrating. For them, a physician mortgage's flexibility — especially around how student loan payments are counted in the debt-to-income (DTI) calculation — becomes even more valuable.

---

## The Silver Lining: Physician Mortgages Still Handle Student Debt Better Than Any Other Loan

Here's the good news: **physician mortgage programs were built with this reality in mind.** Unlike conventional mortgage underwriting, which looks at your full student loan balance or monthly payment at face value, physician loan lenders typically:

- **Exclude deferred student loans** from DTI calculations entirely (policies vary by lender)
- **Use income-driven payment amounts** rather than standard repayment figures in DTI
- **Allow 0% down payments** with no private mortgage insurance, freeing up cash you might otherwise need for a down payment to service debt or build savings

A physician with $280,000 in student loans and a new attending salary might look unqualified under conventional mortgage guidelines. Under a physician mortgage program, that same physician often qualifies for a substantial loan at competitive rates.

## A Few Things Worth Doing Right Now

Given the magnitude of the July 1 changes, here are a few practical steps:

1. **Pull your loan history.** Log into StudentAid.gov and review your loan types, amounts, and employer certification history if you're pursuing PSLF.

2. **Run the numbers on new repayment plans.** The AAMC and StudentLoanPlanner both offer calculators to model how RAP or the Tiered Standard Plan would affect your monthly obligation.

3. **Talk to a physician-focused financial planner.** This isn't the time for generic advice. Advisors who specialize in medical professionals understand how student loan decisions interact with mortgage qualification, tax strategy, and retirement planning.

4. **Get pre-qualified with a physician mortgage lender.** Even if you're not ready to buy yet, knowing what you'd qualify for — and how your student loans factor in — helps you plan. And in a competitive housing market, being pre-qualified puts you ahead.

## The Bottom Line

The July 1, 2026 changes to federal student loan programs are real, significant, and coming soon. For physician home buyers, the main takeaways are: understand how these changes affect your repayment trajectory, recognize that physician mortgage programs offer underwriting flexibility that conventional loans don't, and don't assume the financial plan you built a year ago still reflects current reality.

The housing market is showing signs of improvement heading into summer 2026, with rates trending downward and more lenders competing for physician borrowers. The policy environment is shifting — but for informed buyers, opportunity remains.
