## LinkedIn (professional, insightful)

Most articles about physician mortgages stop at the headline features: 0%
down, no PMI, IBR/PAYE student-loan treatment, employment-contract close.

A quieter underwriting question decides how much house you actually qualify
for: how does the lender count the income that *isn't* base salary?

In 2026, with RVU-heavy contracts standard in hospitalist, anesthesia,
surgical, radiology, and pathology roles, the answer can swing purchasing
power by $50K–$150K on the same file.

The default rule: only guaranteed base salary counts. RVUs, sign-on, call
pay, moonlighting, and locum income require a documented 2-year history
and get averaged across 24 months.

The exception worth shopping for: some physician-loan banks will count a
guaranteed RVU minimum in your contract as base. Others won't. The lender
that says yes can be worth six figures of additional approval.

Three questions worth asking every lender, in writing, before you compare
rates: (1) Base only, base + fraction of expected variable, or base +
24-month average? (2) Any path to count variable income with <24 months of
history? (3) How specifically do you treat sign-on, moonlighting, locums,
and K-1 distributions?

A 0.125% lower rate on $600K is about $50/month. A $150K higher max loan
amount is the difference between a house that fits and one that doesn't.

## Facebook (friendly, accessible)

If you're a doctor, dentist, or pharmacist looking at a physician
mortgage, here's a question that doesn't get asked enough: how does the
lender count the parts of your paycheck that aren't base salary?

RVU bonuses, sign-on bonuses, call pay, moonlighting, locum shifts,
partnership distributions — most lenders will only count your guaranteed
base salary unless you have two full years of documented variable income.

That sounds like fine print. It isn't. On the same exact contract, two
lenders can come back with maximum loan amounts that are $100,000+ apart,
just because one of them counts your guaranteed RVU minimum and the other
won't. That's the difference between the house you actually want and the
one you're settling for.

We wrote a clear, no-jargon walkthrough of how each type of variable
physician income typically gets treated, plus the three questions you
should ask every lender before you compare rates. Worth a read before you
fill out a single application.
