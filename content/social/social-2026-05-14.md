## LinkedIn

**Headline angle: the starter-home playbook quietly broke, and most new attendings haven't done the math.**

The classic advice given to new attendings — "buy a modest starter, trade up in five years" — was built on three assumptions that don't hold in 2026:

1. Rates would be lower at upsize than at purchase.
2. Home prices would compound at 4%–5% a year.
3. Selling and rebuying was cheap.

Today, the Freddie Mac 30-year fixed sits at 6.37% with physician-loan pricing layered 0.125%–0.50% on top. JPMorgan Global Research expects U.S. home prices to stall near 0% in 2026. And selling a $500K starter to buy an $850K forever home runs $55K–$70K in friction costs alone.

There's also a quieter variable physicians are missing: the federal Repayment Assistance Plan (RAP) launches July 1, 2026. For PSLF-track borrowers, the new AGI-based payment formula will likely push DTI *higher* on the next mortgage application than it is today. Translation: the forever home you can qualify for now may be larger than the one you can qualify for in 2029.

Rocket Mortgage's 2026 buyer data tells the same story from the other direction — only 16% of 2025 buyers said they were treating their first home as a tradable starter, down from 29% in 2022.

Decision framework I use with attendings on this question:

→ Will you be in this metro 7+ years? If not, neither answer is right — rent.
→ Is your "forever" budget already contractually visible, or aspirational?
→ How much smaller is the starter, really?
→ Will RAP make your next DTI worse, not better?

For most day-1 attendings with a signed contract and a stable metro, the answer in 2026 is closer to forever than to starter. The friction cost of a fast trade-up is just too high to clear without 4%–5% appreciation doing the work for you.

## Facebook

**Quick reality check for doctors, dentists, and pharmacists thinking about buying this summer:**

The old "buy a starter home and trade up in five years" plan needs a 2026 update.

Mortgage rates are sticky in the mid-6s. Home prices may be flat this year (JPMorgan: ~0% projected). And selling a $500K home to buy an $850K home easily costs $55K–$70K in commissions, closing costs, and a reset amortization clock.

Add the July 1 federal student-loan changes (new RAP plan, AGI-based payments) and the "trade up later" path gets tougher, not easier, for physicians carrying PSLF debt.

If you're a new attending with a signed contract and you plan to stay in your metro for 7+ years — it may make more sense to buy closer to your forever home now, not later. If you don't have that 7-year horizon? Renting is still the underdog answer that keeps proving right.
