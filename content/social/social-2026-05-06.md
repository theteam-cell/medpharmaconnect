## LinkedIn

The 30-year fixed averaged 6.30% the first week of May — about 46 basis points below this time last year. If you closed a physician mortgage in 2024 or 2025 with a rate that starts with a 7, that's enough to make the inbox-noise question real: should you refinance?

Two clocks matter, not one.

The rate clock is the obvious one. On a $500K physician loan, refi closing costs run roughly $10K–$25K. A 0.75% rate drop saves about $250/mo — a 50-month break-even. A 1.0% drop drops break-even to ~38 months. Below 0.50% of improvement, the math almost never works. Above 1.0%, it usually does. The middle is where you actually have to do the arithmetic.

The clock most physicians aren't watching: July 1, 2026. The new Repayment Assistance Plan replaces older income-driven plans and runs 1%–10% of AGI with no payment cap. For a $500K-AGI attending, the top tier is roughly $4,167/mo — well above current IBR or PAYE. Lenders are already pre-underwriting dual DTIs (current IDR vs. projected RAP). A refi that approves today on an $850/mo IDR payment may not approve on a $3,400/mo RAP payment.

For high-earning PSLF attendings with large balances, closing before July 1 may be the cleaner play. For everyone else: run the RAP scenario before you run the rate scenario, because a higher student-loan payment can knock 5%–10% off your borrowing capacity on its own.

A two-minute framework: get one bank quote, one credit union quote, one broker quote. Subtract from your current rate. If the gap is 0.75%+, divide closing costs by monthly savings and compare to your real time-in-house. If the answer is shorter than your stay, refi. If it's longer, set a 60-day alert and wait.

The thaw is real. The math is still the math.

- Same math, different coats — pick the one that fits your time-in-loan
