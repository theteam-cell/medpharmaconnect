## LinkedIn (professional, insightful)

Most physician-mortgage articles obsess over the headline rate. They rarely cover the smaller decision sitting next to it: how — and how long — you lock that rate.

In a calm market, lock mechanics don't move the needle. In May 2026, they do.

Three reasons the lock decision is doing real work right now:

1. **Volatility is back.** Freddie Mac's 30-year survey has bounced between 6.23% and 6.51% in eight weeks. The smoothed weekly print understates how jumpy the daily indices have been.

2. **Lock periods are repriced.** 30- and 45-day locks remain free at most physician-loan banks. 60-day locks now run ~0.125 points. 75- and 90-day locks run 0.25–0.375 points (~$1,250–$1,875 on a $500K loan).

3. **Float-down only earns out under specific conditions.** It typically costs 0.25–0.50 points up front and triggers if rates drop 0.25% or more before closing. On a $500K loan with a 0.375-point cost, you need ~17 months of payments at the lower rate to break even — which is fine if you'll keep the loan that long, useless if you won't.

A practical rule that holds for most physician borrowers:

→ Pick the shortest lock period that comfortably covers your real timeline.
→ Take float-down only when you're locking 45+ days out and the curve is sloping down.
→ Get every lock term — period, fee, float-down trigger, extension policy — in writing on the Loan Estimate.

And the wrinkle specific to this quarter: any file closing after July 1, 2026 will be underwritten against projected Repayment Assistance Plan (RAP) payments rather than current SAVE/PAYE. A lock protects your rate. It does not protect your DTI.

## Facebook (friendly, accessible)

Quick one for the doctors, dentists, and pharmacists shopping for a home this spring.

When you lock your mortgage rate, you're committing to a specific number for 30, 45, 60, or 90 days. Most physician-loan banks make the 30- and 45-day locks free. Anything longer costs a little — usually 0.125 to 0.375 points (that's $625 to $1,875 on a $500K loan).

Two things to keep in mind in May 2026:

1. Rates have been bouncy. The "official" weekly average eased to 6.36% this week, but daily prints have swung 15–20 basis points inside the same week. Pick a lock period that gives you breathing room, but don't pay for 90 days if 45 will do.

2. If your closing falls after July 1, your lender will start using your **projected** new student loan payment (under the new RAP plan) instead of your current one. That can shift your qualifying number meaningfully. A rate lock doesn't shield you from that — only checking with your loan officer ahead of time does.

Three questions worth asking your lender, in writing:

- What does each lock period cost at my loan amount?
- What's your float-down policy (cost, trigger, window)?
- What happens if my closing slips by a few days?
