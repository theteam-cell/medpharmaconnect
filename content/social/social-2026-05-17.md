## LinkedIn (professional, insightful)

Most physician mortgage shopping starts and ends with the rate.

That made sense in 2021, when the 30-year fixed was 2.9% and a quarter-point delta moved the monthly payment more than the entire closing-cost stack.

In spring 2026, with the 30-year fixed clustered at 6.36% and physician-loan dispersion of 25–50 bp across the active banks, the math has flipped. The closing-cost column is where the next several thousand dollars of real savings are now hiding.

Three things changed quietly this cycle:

1. National closing costs rose 3.8% YoY. On a $400K home, buyer-side closing costs now run $8,500–$15,200 — 2% to 5% of purchase price.

2. Seller concessions normalized. About 35% of 2026 transactions include them, averaging ~$8,500 per deal. Two years ago this was rare.

3. Inventory is up materially YoY in many metros, giving qualified physician buyers actual leverage to ask.

For a physician borrower, the rules that catch people off-guard:

— Most physician-loan portfolio programs cap seller concessions at 3% of purchase price above 90% LTV and 6% at or below 75% LTV — but the exact cap is set by each lender's investor guidelines. Confirm in writing.

— Concession dollars apply to closing costs, prepaids, escrows, and discount points. They can't come back as cash and can't pay down principal.

— Concessions can't exceed your actual closing costs. If the cap allows $15K but your true close is $12K, the extra $3K doesn't transfer.

In a buyer-leaning market, the highest-leverage use of seller-credit dollars is almost always a rate buy-down (2-1 or permanent points), not a one-time credit toward the cash-to-close line. The monthly cash flow impact compounds for years.

Read your Loan Estimate line by line. Shop the negotiable fees. Walk into the offer with a specific number — and a specific allocation — for seller credits.

## Facebook (friendly, accessible)

A quick one for doctors, dentists, and pharmacists house-hunting this spring.

Everyone obsesses over the mortgage rate. That makes sense in some markets. In spring 2026, with rates moving in a narrow band, the bigger savings are actually sitting in two places most buyers never look: the closing-cost line and the seller-concession negotiation.

Three things to know before you write your next offer:

✅ Closing costs on a $400K home now run $8,500–$15,200 — about 2%–5% of purchase price.
✅ ~35% of 2026 buyers got seller concessions, averaging ~$8,500 per deal.
✅ Most physician-loan programs let sellers contribute 3%–6% toward your closing costs and points (the exact cap depends on your lender and down payment).

The most valuable thing to ask for? A rate buy-down. A 2-1 buy-down funded by the seller can save you ~$400–$500/month in year one and ~$200–$250/month in year two on a $500K loan. That's real money — and it compounds.

Read your Loan Estimate carefully. Shop your title insurance where you're allowed to. And don't leave seller credits on the table just because no one told you to ask.
