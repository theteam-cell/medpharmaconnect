## LinkedIn (professional, insightful)

The headline feature of a physician mortgage is what it lets you skip: the down payment.

0% down. No PMI. A combination no other product offers at scale.

But here's what the marketing doesn't say: just because you *can* put 0% down doesn't mean you *should*.

With physician-loan rates in the **6.5%–7.0%** band this spring and high-yield savings still paying around **4.25%–4.5%**, the trade-off is sharper than it looks.

A clean rule of thumb: 0% down genuinely wins when you have a higher-yielding use for the cash — paying off 7%+ student loans, maxing tax-advantaged retirement space, or preserving liquidity because you'll likely move in 3–5 years.

10% or 20% down wins when you'll stay 7+ years, your emergency fund is fully built, your higher-rate debt is gone, and the lender's rate gap between LTV tiers is wider than typical (0.50%+).

In practice, most attendings I see end up at **5%–10% down** — capturing most of the rate discount without being all-in on the house.

The takeaway: ask your lender for the *full rate sheet by LTV tier*, not just the rate at your assumed down payment. The math should drive the decision — not the headline.

## Facebook (friendly, accessible)

A question I hear from a lot of doctors, dentists, and pharmacists shopping for their first home:

*"My physician loan lets me put 0% down — should I?"*

Short answer: it depends. And the math is more interesting than you'd think.

Today, physician loans are running about 6.5%–7.0%. A high-yield savings account is paying about 4.25%–4.5%. So if your "down payment cash" is just sitting in HYSA, the mortgage is costing you more than the cash is earning — putting it down can actually beat keeping it.

But if that cash is paying off 7%+ student loans, going into a maxed-out 401(k) match, or sitting as your emergency fund while you're still in your first year as an attending? 0% down is doing its real job — keeping you liquid when life is still in flux.

Most physicians I see split the difference and put 5%–10% down. There's no prize for being all-or-nothing on this.
