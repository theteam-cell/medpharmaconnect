## LinkedIn (professional, insightful)

Physician mortgage advice almost always stops at pre-approval. Sort your DTI, shop three to five lenders, lock a rate — done.

But there's a step between "offer accepted" and "keys in hand" that gets no airtime and can quietly unwind a clean file: the appraisal.

Here's why it bites harder on a physician loan than on almost any other product.

Your lender finances against the LOWER of the contract price or the appraised value. Agree to $700K, appraise at $675K, and the lender treats it as a $675K deal. That $25K is the "appraisal gap."

On a conventional loan with 20% down, a gap is uncomfortable but absorbable — there's already a cash cushion in the deal.

A physician mortgage is built on the opposite premise: 0%–10% down. The same low-down-payment benefit that preserves your cash also removes the shock absorber. When the number comes in low, the gap lands directly on you — as cash you have to find on two weeks' notice.

The good news for 2026: broad appraisal-gap risk is lower than the 2021–22 frenzy — fewer bidding wars, fewer over-list contracts. But the risk is now concentrated rather than universal:

→ Appraisers turn more conservative in a balanced market, leaning on historical comps
→ Years of low transaction volume mean fewer recent comparable sales to work from
→ Risk is intensely local — custom or renovated homes in thin-sales pockets are most exposed

If the number comes in low, you usually have more room than the first panic suggests: keep your appraisal contingency, renegotiate with the seller, request a reconsideration of value, cover the gap in cash, or walk.

The physician loan's low down payment is a real advantage — right up until a low appraisal turns it into exposure. Plan for it instead of being surprised by it.

## Facebook (friendly, accessible)

Buying a home this year? Here's a step that surprises a lot of doctors — and it happens AFTER you're pre-approved.

It's the appraisal. Your lender sends out an independent appraiser to confirm the home is worth what you agreed to pay. And they'll only lend against the lower of your contract price or the appraised value.

So if you agree to $700,000 and it appraises at $675,000, the bank treats it as a $675,000 home. That $25,000 difference? It's on you.

Here's why this matters more on a physician mortgage: the whole point of the product is putting little or no money down. That's great for keeping cash free — but it means there's no cushion to absorb a low appraisal. The gap lands on you as cash, fast.

The good news: in 2026's calmer market, low appraisals are less common than they were a few years ago. And if it does happen, you have options 👇

✅ Keep an appraisal contingency in your offer
✅ Renegotiate — a low appraisal is real evidence the seller will often respect
✅ Ask your lender for a reconsideration of value
✅ Cover the gap in cash if the home is worth it to you
✅ Walk away — with your contingency, you can

Budget a little cash beyond your down payment, and ask your agent early about recent sales in the neighborhood. A little planning makes the appraisal a formality instead of a crisis. 🏡
