## LinkedIn (professional, insightful)

Every summer, thousands of physicians close on a home in a city they've barely visited — sometimes one they've never seen in person.

For a relocating doctor, the hard part usually isn't the financing. It's the choreography. Three things separate a smooth cross-country move from a stressful one:

1. **A clean offer letter, early.** Physician loans can qualify you on a signed contract alone and let you close 60–150 days before your start date. But the letter has to be contingency-free — a pending license or drug screen stops the clock. Resolve those first.

2. **People on the ground you trust.** A physician-savvy local agent and a thorough independent inspection are how you "see" a house from two time zones away. Keep your inspection and appraisal contingencies — with little or nothing down, they're your only cushion.

3. **Honesty about the home you're leaving.** A vacant, unsold home counts against your debt-to-income and shrinks your buying power. Talk to your lender before you list. And if the new city is unfamiliar, renting for a year isn't a failure of nerve — it's often the smartest move.

Rates hit 6.53% last week, the highest in nine months. But for a relocating physician, the rate is rarely the deciding factor. The choreography is.

## Facebook (friendly, accessible)

Moving across the country for a new medical job this summer? You might end up closing on a house before you've ever set foot in the city — and that's more normal than it sounds. 🏡

The trick is the choreography: get a clean, contingency-free offer letter early (physician loans let you close up to 150 days before your start date), hire a local agent and inspector you trust to be your eyes, and keep your contingencies since a 0%-down loan leaves no cushion for surprises.

And here's the one most people get wrong — the home you're *leaving* affects how much you qualify for on the new one. Talk to your lender before you list.
