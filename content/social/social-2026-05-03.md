## LinkedIn (professional, insightful)

"No PMI" is the headline of every physician-mortgage pitch I see. It's a real advantage. It's also smaller than the brochure suggests once you run the full math.

The honest version, for doctors / dentists / pharmacists shopping in spring 2026:

→ PMI on 10%-down conventional for a 740 FICO borrower: roughly $130–$220/month, and federally required to auto-cancel at 78% LTV — typically 8–10 years on a 30-year amortization.

→ Physician-mortgage rates currently run +0.125% to +0.50% over comparable conventional. On a $540K loan, every 25 bp of rate ≈ $70–$80/month — and that premium doesn't expire when you cross 80% LTV. It runs the full 30 years unless you refinance.

→ Physician mortgage clearly wins when: you're putting <10% down, you're in jumbo territory, or student-loan DTI is squeezing you.

→ Conventional + PMI can quietly edge ahead when: you can put 20% down, you have a 760+ FICO with a fat physician-mortgage spread quoted, or LPMI is available at sharp pricing.

The decision is a one-line spreadsheet:
(Rate-premium $/month × years until you'd refinance) vs. (PMI $/month × years until 80% LTV).

Whichever total is smaller is the cheaper loan. The "no PMI" line is true. It just isn't the whole sentence.
