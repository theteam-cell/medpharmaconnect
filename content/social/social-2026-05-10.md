## LinkedIn (professional, insightful)

Most physician mortgages are designed for single-family detached homes. Once you shop a condo, the loan starts to behave differently — and often quietly.

Three things doctors, dentists, and pharmacists in condo-heavy metros (Boston, Miami, DC, Chicago, San Diego, Seattle, NYC) are running into in spring 2026:

1. Many physician programs now add a 0.125% rate adjuster and tighten LTV by 5–10% on warrantable condos. Non-warrantable condos are typically capped at 90% LTV — so the "0% down" headline doesn't apply.

2. The building itself has to clear warrantability — reserves above ~10% of the operating budget, owner-occupancy above 50%, no active litigation, adequate master insurance, no pending special assessments. Your unit can be perfect; the building can still kill the loan.

3. HOA dues count as housing expense for DTI. In Miami-Dade, condo dues are approaching $900/mo. A $700/mo HOA can shave $80K–$100K off the price you qualify for vs. a single-family home with the same monthly mortgage.

Three questions to ask every lender, in writing, before you make an offer:
— Is this property type at par with single-family on your physician program?
— Do you require a full condo questionnaire, and how long does it take in this metro?
— Are there any deal-breakers you can flag from the listing or county records before I go under contract?

Educational only. Not a lender.

## Facebook (friendly, accessible)

If you're a doctor, dentist, or pharmacist looking at condos this spring, here's something most physician-loan ads don't tell you up front:

The loan you saw advertised was probably built around a single-family home. Once you switch to a condo or non-warrantable building, the rate, the down payment, and even whether the deal closes can all change.

Things that quietly trip up condo deals in 2026:
• Rate adjusters around 0.125% on condos that aren't there for single-family homes
• A 90% LTV cap on non-warrantable condos (so 10% down minimum)
• HOA dues — averaging $500–$800/mo across many Florida metros, $400–$500+ in newer Carolinas mid-rises — that count toward your DTI
• Pending special assessments or low building reserves that push the unit out of warrantability after you're under contract

The good news: most of this is knowable before you make an offer. We put together a short read on what to ask both your lender and the listing agent before you sign anything.
