# Research Notes — 2026-04-28

## 1. Physician mortgage rates: pricing tiers re-stratifying around credit score as lender competition heats up

Late-April 2026 quotes still cluster in the **6.5%–7.0% band** for 30-year fixed physician mortgages, with the "doctor tax" (premium over conventional) running **0.125%–0.50%**. What is shifting is *how* lenders are pricing inside that band:

- Several large physician-loan programs are publicly tying loan limits and down-payment tiers to FICO. Representative bracketing seen this week: **100% financing up to ~$1.5M at a 680 FICO**, stepping up to **~$2.0M at 720+**, with the best rate sheet typically reserved for **740+**.
- Industry guidance frequently cited: roughly **0.125% rate improvement for every ~20 FICO points above 740**, up to lender-specific caps (often 780).
- Because non-bank lenders entering the physician space are repricing more aggressively at the high-FICO end, the *gap between "qualifying" and "best-priced"* applicants has widened. A 700-FICO file may get approved at a 0.375%–0.50% premium that a 760-FICO file does not pay.

Practical implication for the MedPharmaConnect audience: small, deliberate credit-score moves in the **3–6 months before applying** can be worth more in 2026 than they were in 2024–2025, because lenders are leaning harder on the rate sheet to differentiate.

## 2. July 1, 2026 student loan transition — ~9 weeks out, RAP details and PSLF residency-credit ambiguity

Two developments worth flagging this week as the **July 1, 2026** federal loan changes approach:

- **RAP details are firming up.** For new borrowers after July 1, RAP will be the only IDR plan and the only IDR plan PSLF-eligible. It includes **unpaid-interest relief** and a **$50/month principal reduction** when the calculated payment doesn't cover interest — useful guardrails — but RAP terms can extend up to **30 years** for medical school borrowers and there is **no payment cap**, which means attending-stage payments can run materially higher than under prior IDR plans.
- **PSLF residency-credit treatment remains the most-watched item.** Coverage this spring is mixed: some sources continue to report that residency/fellowship months count toward the 120 PSLF payments after July 1; others, including AAMC-aligned commentary, flag legislative and regulatory language that could exclude training years for borrowers who originate after the cutoff. **The conservative reading for any resident or attending mortgage borrower is to confirm current PSLF status and IDR payment in writing with their servicer now**, since DTI calculations on a physician mortgage often lean on the documented IDR figure.

Existing borrowers (pre-July-1, 2026 disbursements) remain grandfathered for the time being, with a hard cutover deadline of **July 1, 2028** for borrowers currently on SAVE, PAYE, or ICR.

## 3. Spring 2026 housing market — buyer leverage strongest since 2019, but high earners face nuanced rent-vs.-buy math

The April reads on the spring market continue to favor qualified buyers more than any time since 2019:

- Forecasts cluster around **2026 home-price growth of 2%–3%** with **wage growth running ~4%** — the first year in five where pay outpaces prices nationally.
- Median monthly mortgage payments are running **down ~5% year-over-year** even with rates in the mid-6s, driven by softer prices in formerly hot markets (Austin, parts of Florida, parts of California).
- For high-income earners specifically, more recent buyer surveys note that **flexibility is being priced into the rent-vs.-buy decision** more explicitly: residents and early-career attendings are increasingly opting to rent for 1–3 years post-training rather than commit to a 30-year payment at 6.5%+, especially in markets where the buy-vs.-rent monthly delta is still 25%+ in favor of renting.

For the MedPharmaConnect audience, the takeaway is that **spring 2026 is a real opportunity for the right buyer**, but the right buyer is one with a stable post-training location, a clean credit profile, and a documented student-loan payment — not a buyer rushing in on the assumption that rates will return to a pandemic-era 3% handle.
