# Research Notes — 2026-04-20

## Item 1: 30-year fixed mortgage rates easing toward 6% in spring 2026

As of April 16, 2026, the 30-year fixed-rate mortgage averaged 6.30%, down from 6.37% the prior week and from 6.83% one year ago. Fannie Mae's March 2026 forecast projects rates will drop below 6% for the remainder of 2026, with a year-end target near 5.7%. Affordability has now improved for eight consecutive months, and inventory has reached 1.23M homes (+4.2% YoY) while home prices have risen just 0.4% YoY. Physician mortgage rates are typically 0.25%–0.75% above conventional/jumbo products, so doctors can expect current rates in the 6.5%–7.0% range with similar directional easing expected through year-end.

**Why it matters for MedPharmaConnect readers:** Residents and attendings who were priced out in 2023–2024 may find spring/summer 2026 to be the most favorable buying window in several years — softer rates plus loosening inventory.

Source: Freddie Mac PMMS; Fannie Mae March 2026 forecast; Redfin spring 2026 data.

## Item 2: Non-banks expanding into the physician mortgage niche

Industry reporting indicates a growing number of non-bank lenders are entering the doctor-loan space alongside traditional portfolio banks. Several programs are also broadening eligibility beyond MDs/DOs/DDSs to include CRNAs, nurse practitioners, physician assistants, and in some cases pharmacists. Shopping across 3–5 lenders now reveals rate differences of 0.25%–0.50% on identical borrower profiles, because physician mortgages are portfolio-priced rather than sold to Fannie/Freddie.

**Why it matters:** More lender competition = better leverage for borrowers. The "only one bank in town does doctor loans" era is over. Readers should be coached to shop aggressively.

Source: National Mortgage News; LeverageRx 2026 lender roundup.

## Item 3: Student loan DTI treatment remains the decisive advantage

Physician mortgage programs continue to use actual IDR/IBR monthly payments (or exclude loans in 12+ month deferment) when calculating debt-to-income — rather than the 1% of balance or fully amortized payment that conventional underwriters often impute. For a physician with $250K in student debt on SAVE/PAYE, this can be the difference between a DTI that qualifies and one that does not, especially for residents and early-career attendings. This underwriting flexibility, combined with 0% down options up to ~$1M–$2M and no PMI, remains the single biggest reason the product exists.

**Why it matters:** Worth a reminder article every quarter — most new residents still don't know how radically different physician-loan DTI math is from conventional.

Source: White Coat Investor; Physician Loans USA; SalaryDr 2026 guide.
