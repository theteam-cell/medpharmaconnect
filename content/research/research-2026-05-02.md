# Research Notes — 2026-05-02

## 1. Rate environment at the start of May 2026 — physician loans still mid-6% to low-7%

- Industry consensus heading into May puts the 30-yr fixed physician mortgage in the **mid-6% to low-7%** band, with a **0.125%–0.50% premium** over comparable conventional. CBS News' May 1, 2026 daily mortgage rate roundup pegs conventional 30-yr fixed in the low-6% range, putting the typical physician-loan quote at **~6.50%–6.875%** for well-qualified files.
- Forecast bias for the rest of 2026 is **modestly lower** if inflation cools, with most desks projecting a 5.9%–6.5% range for conventional 30-yr through year-end. The practical refi threshold for a physician borrower locking today at ~6.75% is conventional rates near or below **6.0%** — a level the Street doesn't expect before late 2026 or early 2027.
- Across physician-loan lenders, the spread between cheapest and most expensive quote on the **same file** is still **0.25%–0.50%**. On a $600K loan over 30 years that's $25K–$50K+ in lifetime interest — the same magnitude as a 5%-vs-20% down payment swing, which makes today's article (down payment strategy) a natural pair with last week's "shop 5 lenders" piece.

## 2. Spring 2026 housing-market mood: cautious but active, regional split

- Coldwell Banker's 2026 Home Shopping Season Report (700+ agents nationwide) finds **43% of agents** reporting a busier spring than 2025 and **80% of agents** saying buyers are *not* waiting for further rate drops. Translation for the physician audience: the "wait for 5% rates" crowd has largely capitulated.
- Regional split is sharp:
  - **Sellers' markets**: 70% of Midwest agents and 74% of Northeast agents
  - **Buyers' markets**: 56% of South agents, 46% of West agents
  - This matters for a physician matching/relocating: Sun Belt and West Coast metros (Tampa, Phoenix, Sarasota, Austin, Denver, Seattle) skew buyer-friendly with price cuts and longer DOM; Midwest/Northeast (Columbus, Indianapolis, Boston suburbs, NJ) stay competitive.
- National price growth projected **+2% to +4%** for 2026 — slowest in years — meaning the **opportunity cost of putting 20% down vs. 0% down** is less about home appreciation outpacing brokerage returns and more about fixed-income alpha (HYSA/T-bills 4.25%–4.5%) vs. mortgage-rate-cost (~6.5%) net of tax.

## 3. Student-loan policy: ~8.5 weeks to July 1, 2026 RAP transition

- The **July 1, 2026 RAP cutover** is now roughly **8.5 weeks away** (60 days). Key impacts confirmed by recent reporting:
  - **RAP becomes the only IDR plan for loans disbursed on/after July 1, 2026.** Payment formula: **1%–10% of AGI** (not discretionary income), $10/mo minimum, forgiveness at 30 years.
  - **No payment cap under RAP** — a critical change vs. IBR. A physician earning $500K AGI on RAP could face a payment near **$4,167/month**, vs. an IBR-capped Standard 10-yr equivalent.
  - **Residency/fellowship years no longer count toward PSLF** for new borrowers post-July 1.
  - Existing borrowers preserve **IBR access** — but only as long as they don't consolidate, take out a new loan, or otherwise trigger a re-disbursement.
- Mortgage-DTI implication for physicians shopping in May/June: lenders are increasingly quoting **two DTIs** — current IBR-capped, and post-July-1 RAP-equivalent — to stress-test approval. Borrowers with thin DTI margin may want to **lock and close pre-July 1** to avoid a re-underwrite surprise.

## Implications for today's article

- The combination of (a) physician-loan rates in the 6.5%–7.0% band, (b) HYSA/T-bills returning 4.25%–4.5%, (c) home-price growth slowing to 2%–4%, and (d) physician loans uniquely allowing **0% down with no PMI** creates a clean teaching moment about **down payment strategy**.
- The right answer isn't always 0% (the headline feature) and it isn't always 20% (the "responsible" default). It depends on the math: opportunity cost of cash, emergency-fund adequacy, expected hold period, marginal tax rate, and the specific rate gap between the 0%-down and 10/20%-down tiers a lender quotes.
- Article should give doctors, dentists, and pharmacists a **decision framework** with specific dollar examples — not a one-size-fits-all "always put 0% down because you're a doctor" pitch.
