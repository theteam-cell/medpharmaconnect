# Research Notes — 2026-04-27

## 1. Physician mortgage rates holding mid-6% with widening lender spread

Industry trackers continue to peg 30-year fixed physician mortgage rates in the **6.5%–7.0% range** for most borrowers in late April 2026, with most analysts projecting the band will hold through year-end and only modestly drift below 6% by Q4. The "doctor tax" — the rate premium over comparable conventional loans — is consistently quoted at **0.125%–0.50%**, working out to roughly **$25,000–$30,000 in extra lifetime interest on a $500,000 loan**.

The new wrinkle this week: the **lender-to-lender spread on physician programs is widening** as more non-bank lenders enter the space. Borrowers shopping three lenders are seeing rate quotes vary by as much as 0.625% on identical credit profiles, versus historical norms closer to 0.25%. Practical implication for our audience: shopping pays more in physician loans than in any other loan product right now, and "I called my hospital's preferred lender" is increasingly an expensive shortcut.

## 2. July 1, 2026 student loan transition now ~9 weeks away — RAP becomes the only IDR for new borrowers

The OBBBA-driven federal student loan changes hit on **July 1, 2026** — about nine weeks from today. Headline impacts for medical professionals:

- **Grad PLUS eliminated for new borrowers**, with grad/professional students capped at **$50,000/year** in federal Unsubsidized Loans and **$200,000 lifetime** for professional degrees.
- **The new Repayment Assistance Plan (RAP) becomes the only IDR option** for loans disbursed on or after July 1, 2026. Unlike SAVE, RAP has **no payment cap**, meaning attending physicians' eventual payments under RAP can run materially higher than they would have under prior IDR plans.
- **Existing borrowers get grandfathered.** Anyone who took a Federal Direct Loan before July 1, 2026 keeps current options through graduation or three additional academic years, whichever comes first.
- **PSLF residency credit was preserved** in the final bill — earlier draft language excluding training years was dropped — but the Department of Education gains new authority to disqualify specific employers from PSLF eligibility starting that date.

Practical implication for the physician mortgage audience: residents on existing IDR plans should **document their current monthly payment** with their servicer in writing now, because lenders will use that figure for DTI well into 2026 and 2027 even if servicer systems get noisy after the transition.

## 3. Spring 2026 housing market — wage growth outpacing prices, but high-earner math is regional

Macro signals for the spring buying season are notably more buyer-friendly than 2025:

- **Home sales projected up ~14% nationwide in 2026**, with home-price growth of just **2%–3%** — the first year in five where wage growth is meaningfully outpacing price growth.
- **Median monthly mortgage payment is ~$2,559, down nearly 5% YoY**, while wages are up ~4%. That's a real affordability tailwind for first-time buyers.
- **Regional divergence remains sharp.** High-cost coastal markets (Austin, Miami, parts of California) are seeing softening prices and longer days-on-market; Midwestern metros (Columbus, Indianapolis, Kansas City) are tighter than the headlines suggest.
- For high-income earners specifically, the rent-vs.-buy math is genuinely close in many large metros — Redfin and others note a growing share of high earners *choosing* to rent for flexibility rather than locking into 30-year payments at 6.5%+.

For the MedPharmaConnect audience, the takeaway is that the spring 2026 market gives qualified physician buyers **more leverage than they've had in five years** — but the right answer is highly local, and the "buy ASAP because rates will keep falling" narrative is overstated. Forecasts cluster around a 6%-handle for the rest of 2026, not a return to the pandemic-era 3% world.
