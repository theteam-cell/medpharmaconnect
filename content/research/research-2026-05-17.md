# Research Notes — 2026-05-17

## 1. Rate backdrop: 30-yr fixed eases another bp to 6.36%; physician-loan band still mid-6% to low-7%

Freddie Mac's PMMS for the week ending May 14, 2026 reports the 30-year fixed at **6.36%**, down 1 bp from 6.37% the prior week and **~45 bp below spring 2025** (6.81% a year ago). The 15-year fixed eased to **5.71%** (from 5.72%). Daily indices remain volatile in the **6.30%–6.51%** range over the last 10 sessions.

For physician mortgages specifically, the practical band still sits **mid-6% to low-7%** with the familiar two layers of dispersion on identical files:

- **0.125%–0.50% premium** over conventional ("doctor tax")
- **0.25%–0.50% lender dispersion** across the 5–7 active physician-loan banks

Sell-side rate consensus for May–July remains **5.9%–6.5%** with practical refi threshold for spring-2024/2025 originations not expected before late-2026/early-2027.

**Why it matters for today's article on closing costs:** Rate-driven shopping has been the focus all spring. As rates stabilize in a narrow band, the **closing-cost line** is where the next 25–50 bp of real-dollar savings is actually hiding — buyers focused only on rate are leaving meaningful money on the table.

## 2. Closing costs are rising into a buyer-leaning market — and seller concessions are quietly normalizing at $8,500

National benchmarks reported by industry trackers for 2026:

- Average closing costs **rose 3.8% YoY** into 2026; on a **$350K home**, base closing costs (excluding prepaids) now average ~**$6,900**.
- On a **$400K home** (closer to the median attending-buyer transaction), buyer closing costs now run **$8,500–$15,200**, or **2%–5%** of purchase price.
- Cost stack roughly: **$3,500–$6,000 lender fees** (origination, underwriting, processing), **$2,000–$4,000 third-party** (appraisal, title, survey, inspection), and **$3,000–$5,200 prepaids/escrow** (taxes, insurance, prepaid interest, escrow funding).
- **~35% of buyers received seller concessions** in 2026's market, averaging **~$8,500** per deal.
- Inventory is up materially YoY in many metros (industry sources cite 8%–15% depending on cut), so seller-concession leverage is real where it wasn't 18 months ago.

Concession caps by loan type (conventional benchmarks): FHA up to 6%, VA up to 4%, USDA up to 6%. **Physician mortgage programs (portfolio products) generally allow seller concessions up to 3% for ≤90% LTV and up to 6% for ≤75% LTV, but the cap is set by the individual lender's investor guidelines and must be confirmed in writing.**

**Why it matters for today's article:** Closing costs are the most ignored lever in physician-mortgage shopping. With seller concessions normalizing at $8,500/deal and the spring 2026 housing market tilting buyer-friendly in 4 of 7 major metros, this is the cycle where physician borrowers can compress effective closing costs meaningfully — without trading down on the loan.

## 3. SAVE wind-down notices begin in 6.5 weeks; RAP underwriting is now standard at physician-loan banks

Department of Education guidance confirms that **federal loan servicers will begin issuing 90-day transition notices on July 1, 2026** to borrowers still in SAVE. Borrowers who don't actively elect a plan within 90 days are **auto-enrolled into Standard or the new Tiered Standard**.

For physicians specifically, the Medical Economics 2026 guide highlights three buckets of impact:

- **New borrowing limits** for medical students (~$50K/yr / $200K total professional-student cap)
- **Narrower IDR menu** for new borrowers: RAP only; IBR remains available for those with pre-July-2026 loans
- **PSLF eligibility tightened** with employer-disqualification authority

The dollar swing matters: a physician with **$200K in pre-July-2026 loans** on **IBR** pays roughly **$2,000/month** (capped); the same borrower on **RAP** pays about **$4,167/month** (no cap, AGI-based 1%–10%).

**Why it matters for today's article:** Closing costs and student-loan plan choice intersect when seller-concession dollars exceed the buyer's allowable use. Concessions can pay closing costs, prepaids, and discount points, but **cannot go to the borrower as cash and cannot pay down principal**. For physicians juggling RAP-uncapped payments after July 1, allocating concession dollars to a **rate buy-down or escrow funding** rather than basic closing costs often beats the headline trade.
