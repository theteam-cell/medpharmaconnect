# Research Notes — 2026-04-23

## Item 1: Mortgage rates ticked up this week on geopolitical and inflation pressure

After two weeks of gentle decline, the 30-year fixed conventional rate moved higher mid-week. U.S. News's daily tracker put the average 30-year purchase rate at 6.387% on April 22, 2026, with the 30-year refinance rate at 6.478% and the 15-year at 5.477%. That is above last week's Freddie Mac PMMS average of 6.30% on April 16. The driver is a familiar one: March CPI printed at 3.3% year-over-year — the hottest inflation reading since April 2024 — and oil prices have stayed elevated on continued Middle East tensions, both of which feed through to the 10-year Treasury and to mortgage pricing.

**Why it matters for MedPharmaConnect readers:** The narrative of a smooth rate descent toward the mid-5s is back on shakier ground. Physician-loan pricing (portfolio, 0.125%–0.50% above agency) is currently in the 6.50%–7.10% range. The content emphasis for April/May should continue to lean on rate *shopping* as the highest-leverage lever, not on rate *timing*.

## Item 2: Non-bank lenders are entering the physician mortgage market at scale

Two meaningful shifts in the supply side of the physician-mortgage market have landed in the last four months. Redwood Trust launched a medical professionals loan program in December 2025 and has already issued the first RMBS 2.0-era prime private-label securitization of medical-professional loans through its Sequoia jumbo conduit, with ratings from both Fitch and KBRA. Newrez (Rithm's mortgage unit) launched its own Medical Professional Home Loan across retail, direct-to-consumer, wholesale, and joint-venture channels. KBRA now estimates that medical-professional loan production entering prime private-label channels could reach roughly $5 billion annually.

The practical consequence: for the first time in the product's history, physicians, dentists, and pharmacists are being served not only by bank portfolio programs (BMO, Fifth Third, Truist, TD, First Citizens) but also by securitization-backed non-bank originators. That tends to mean more rate competition, more loan-size headroom, and broader professional eligibility — CRNAs, NPs, and PAs are showing up in the fine print more often.

**Why it matters:** The lender-shopping message we've been giving readers ("get 3–5 quotes in the same week") just got more concrete. The three-to-five pool is no longer drawn from the same handful of regional banks; it now includes non-bank originators with meaningfully different pricing logic. Worth reflecting in upcoming articles.

Source: National Mortgage News — "Non-banks see opportunity in physician mortgage niche"; Student Loan Planner — "18 Best Physician Home Loans of 2026"; SalaryDr — "Physician Mortgage Loans: 2026 Complete Guide."

## Item 3: The July 1, 2026 federal student loan changes are 10 weeks away

This is a follow-up to 04-21's research note, but the timeline has compressed meaningfully. As of today we are 69 days from the July 1 effective date of the two major scheduled changes: (1) residency and fellowship years will no longer count toward PSLF for loans first disbursed on or after July 1, 2026, and (2) Graduate PLUS loans will be eliminated, with future graduate/professional borrowers capped at $50,000/year in unsubsidized Stafford and a $200,000 lifetime cap for professional degrees. Separately, existing income-driven plans (SAVE, PAYE, and some ICR/IBR versions) are scheduled to sunset by July 2028. Borrowers who took loans before July 1, 2026 are protected under the current rules, but the political environment has kept the window-for-uncertainty alive.

**Why it matters:** Pharmacists are affected by this too — PharmDs working for qualifying nonprofits (hospitals, public health) have historically relied on PSLF, and the $200K professional-degree lifetime cap does cover the median PharmD debt load (~$170K–$180K) but leaves little room for cost-of-attendance growth. Worth flagging in any 2026 pharmacist-focused content.
