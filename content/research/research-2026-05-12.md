# Research Notes — 2026-05-12

## 1) Rates: 30-yr fixed steady at 6.37%, physician-loan band still mid-6% to low-7%

Freddie Mac PMMS for the week ending May 7, 2026 shows the 30-year fixed-rate
mortgage at **6.37%**, the second consecutive uptick after a 6.23% reading two
weeks prior — but still **~39 bp below spring 2025**. Industry consensus for the
May–July window remains a 5.9%–6.5% band as oil-driven CPI volatility plays
against decelerating shelter inflation.

Physician-loan pricing continues to run **6.5%–7.0%** for 30-yr fixed
portfolio product, with the familiar **+0.125% to +0.50%** premium over
conventional and **0.25%–0.50% lender dispersion** on identical files. Basel
III phase-ins on bank capital are still cited by industry contacts as a reason
the physician spread is not compressing back to its 2023 norm.

## 2) Jumbo physician mortgage product expanding as 2026 conforming limits reset

The 2026 baseline **conforming loan limit** is **$832,750** for a one-unit
home, with the high-cost-area ceiling at **$1,249,125**. Anything above those
thresholds drops into jumbo territory — and the physician-loan product has
quietly become one of the cleanest jumbo paths for high-earning medical
professionals.

Key data points and current observations:

- **KeyBank's Professional Mortgage Program** publishes a tiered structure
  with loan limits up to **$3.5M**: 0% down to $1M, 10% down for $1M–$2M, and
  20% down for $2M–$3.5M. BMO, Bank of America Doctor Loan, Fifth Third, and
  several regional portfolio lenders publish similar tiered ladders in the
  $1.5M–$3M range.
- In **most California metros** where median prices exceed the conforming
  ceiling, physician programs avoid both the conventional jumbo qualifying
  stack and PMI on lower down payments.
- Physician programs typically underwrite to **the bank's portfolio
  guidelines** rather than agency rules — that is the source of both the
  flexibility (IDR student loans, future income, no PMI) and the price
  (~12.5–50 bp rate premium, tighter reserves at the top tiers).
- The under-discussed risk on jumbo physician files: **single-investor
  concentration**. Portfolio lenders can pull or re-price the product
  mid-pipeline, which matters more on a $1.5M file than a $450K file.

## 3) RAP cutover now ~7 weeks out; jumbo files are the most rate-sensitive to dual-DTI underwriting

The **Repayment Assistance Plan (RAP)** becomes the sole IDR option for new
Direct Loan borrowers on **July 1, 2026**, and an option (alongside existing
IDR plans) for current borrowers. Per the RAP rule:

- Monthly payments are calculated against **total AGI**, not discretionary
  income, on a sliding scale of **1%–10%** for AGIs above $10,000.
- There is **no payment cap** under RAP, in contrast to most of the legacy IDR
  plans.
- Forgiveness moves to **30 years** regardless of degree level.
- **PSLF remains available**, but RAP payments count differently than legacy
  IDR payments for residency credit; the implementing guidance is still
  being finalized.

For mortgage underwriting, the practical issue is that physician-loan banks
are increasingly **pre-underwriting projected RAP payments** for any borrower
likely to be on RAP at closing — particularly attendings with high balances.
On a $500K AGI file, the difference between an IDR payment of ~$0 and an RAP
payment of **~$4,167/mo** can swing the qualifying purchase price by
**$80K–$120K**. That swing is rarely material on a $450K file but is
frequently decisive on a $1.5M+ jumbo physician file, where DTI is closer to
the program ceiling to begin with.

## Implications for today's article

Jumbo physician mortgages — the $1M+ product — sit at the intersection of all
three: rate sensitivity (the spread compounds on big balances), program
structure (tiered down payment ladders unique to the physician product), and
RAP dual-DTI risk (the biggest swing factor on the highest-leverage files).
Today's article focuses on the jumbo physician mortgage as a distinct product
and decision framework, not just a bigger version of the standard physician
loan.
