# Research Notes — 2026-05-10 (Sunday)

## 1. Property-type adjusters still a hidden cost on physician mortgages
- The physician-loan band remains mid-6% to low-7% with a 0.125%–0.50% premium over conventional and 0.25%–0.50% lender dispersion on identical files.
- Single-family detached homes generally get the best pricing. Warrantable condos usually price at par, but several physician-loan banks now add a 0.125% rate adjuster for condominiums.
- Non-warrantable condos are still allowed by many physician programs but are typically capped at 90% LTV (10% down minimum) — a meaningful reset from the 0%–5%-down headline most doctors expect.
- 2–4 unit properties, mixed-use, and high-rise condos in concentration markets often get knocked down further (LTV cuts, rate add-ons, or program ineligibility).
- Practical takeaway: the property type can change the deal more than the FICO bracket. Doctors shopping condos should ask each lender for the LE on the same property and compare LTV cap, rate, and any condo questionnaire fee — three line items, not just rate.

Source: WealthKeel, "Physician Mortgage Loans 2026: 0% Down, No PMI Guide for Doctors"; DrLoanTool, "Physician Mortgage Rates 2026."

## 2. HOA dues and condo insurance keep grinding up — and it shows up in DTI
- Florida metro condos: HOA dues averaging $500–$800/mo, with Miami-Dade up from $567 to $900/mo over five years.
- Charlotte, Greensboro, Raleigh: $400–$500+ HOA dues becoming common in newer mid-rise buildings.
- HOA insurance premiums in 2026 range from $2,400 to $75,000+/yr; industry projections call for ~8% homeowners insurance increases in both 2026 and 2027, with disaster-exposed associations seeing 30%–500% spikes. HOAs are passing those through as 10%–20% annual fee increases.
- Why this matters for physician mortgages: HOA dues are added to the housing payment for DTI. A $700/mo HOA on a $750K condo can shave roughly $80K–$100K off the price a doctor qualifies for vs. the same loan on a single-family home.
- Lenders also scrutinize special assessments. Buildings with active or recent assessments above ~5% of unit value, or with reserves under 10% of operating budget, often fail the warrantability test.

Sources: Florida Realty Marketplace, "Average HOA Fees in Florida Compared (2026)"; Pro Insurance Group, "HOA Insurance Cost 2026"; HOA Mgt Company, "Top HOA Issues for 2026."

## 3. July 1, 2026 RAP cutover now ~7.5 weeks out — residency credit risk is sharpening
- New AAMC/Curbside guidance is consistent: for new borrowers on or after July 1, 2026, residency and fellowship years would no longer count toward the 120 PSLF payments, and RAP will be the only IDR plan eligible for PSLF for new borrowers.
- For current borrowers, PAYE, SAVE, and ICR sunset by July 2028, with most rolling into RAP unless they actively choose IBR. RAP runs 1%–10% of AGI with no payment cap for high earners — meaningfully higher than current PAYE/IBR for many attendings.
- Mortgage-side ripple: more lenders are pre-underwriting student loans at the projected RAP payment to stress-test files closing in May–July, even when current statements show lower numbers. Two doctors with the same balance can now get different DTIs depending on which lender models RAP and which still uses statement payment.
- Practical takeaway: any doctor with a high federal student-loan balance who is closing this summer should ask their loan officer in writing which payment number is being used in the underwriting model.

Sources: AAMC, "Preparing for Upcoming Student Loan Changes"; Student Loan Planner, "How the RAP Plan Changes Repayment and PSLF for Medical Residents"; Curbside Real Estate, "PSLF is Changing in 2026"; Tate Esq., "Student Loan Changes on July 1, 2026."
