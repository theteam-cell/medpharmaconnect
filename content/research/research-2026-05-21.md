## 2. Student-loan policy (RAP / SAVE wind-down) — July 1, 2026

- **~6 weeks out** to the July 1 RAP cutover (41 days from today).
- **SAVE wind-down mechanics (Earnest, Dept. of Education, NerdWallet, SoFi, Money, Congress.gov CRS):** starting **July 1**, servicers begin issuing notices instructing SAVE-enrolled borrowers to exit and enroll in a legal plan within **90 days**. Borrowers who miss the window are **auto-enrolled into the Standard Repayment Plan or the new Tiered Standard Plan**.
- **RAP mechanics:** payments run **1%–10% of AGI**, scaling with income; **$10/mo minimum**; each dependent reduces the payment. RAP shields on-time payers from runaway interest and matches unpaid principal to keep the balance moving down. For anyone borrowing **on or after July 1, 2026**, RAP is the **only IDR plan**.
- **Doctor-specific caveat (confirmed by Dept. of Education language):** borrowers with **very high debt-to-income ratios — explicitly including professional-degree borrowers** — who would otherwise reach the maximum repayment period under any IDR plan **may pay more out of pocket over the life of the loan under RAP**. This is the cleanest official confirmation yet that the new plan is not uniformly cheaper for high-balance physicians and dentists.
- **Mortgage-side ripple:** physician lenders continue to **pre-underwrite projected RAP payments** on any file closing after July 1. Because RAP's percentage-of-AGI payment is uncapped, the qualifying-DTI swing is largest on high-AGI, high-balance files.

## 3. Topic-of-the-day signal: the home appraisal on a physician mortgage

Housing and appraisal sources (HousingWire, HomeLight, The Mortgage Reports, AmeriSave, McKissock) flag the **appraisal step** as a 2026 wrinkle that physician homebuyers under-plan for. Most physician-loan coverage stops at pre-approval; the appraisal is where a clean file can still hit a snag. Worth a dedicated article.

Reported 2026 details:

- **"The appraisal gap in 2026" (HousingWire):** the gap dynamic has shifted from prior years. In a **balanced/stabilizing market**, appraisers turn **more conservative**, leaning on **historical sales data** that may not capture current demand or a home's unique features — which can produce **more frequent low appraisals** in specific pockets.
- **Thin-comp problem:** years of elevated rates suppressed transaction volume, so there are **fewer recent comparable sales** for appraisers to work from — less data, less precise valuations, higher gap odds.
- **Geographic divergence:** geography is now one of the most important variables — newer-home markets that once boomed are slowing while others strengthen, so appraisal risk is **highly local**.
- **Climate/insurance overlay:** in high-risk markets where insurers have pulled out, transaction volume has dwindled further, leaving appraisers using **older, outdated comps** — a notable source of low appraisals in those areas.
- **Counter-trend:** broadly, low appraisals are **less common in the slower 2026 market** than during the 2021–2022 frenzy — moderating price growth and reduced bidding competition mean fewer aggressive over-list contracts to begin with. The risk is concentrated, not universal.
- **Physician-loan angle:** the lender orders the appraisal to confirm value before underwriting clears. On a high-LTV physician loan (0%–10% down), a low appraisal bites harder — there is little or no down-payment cushion to absorb a value shortfall, so the borrower must cover the gap in cash, renegotiate, or walk. **Appraisal waivers** (automated valuation in lieu of a full appraisal) appear on some physician-loan files but are not guaranteed, especially on jumbo amounts.

**Today's article topic: the appraisal step on a physician mortgage — why a low appraisal hits a 0%-down file harder, where 2026 appraisal-gap risk is concentrated, and a practical playbook for what to do if the number comes in low.**

- April 2026 saw **lower home-buying activity** as rising rates pinched affordability; inventory increased and **annual home values fell in nearly half of major U.S. metros** (Zillow).
- Spring 2026 reads as **balanced — neither surging nor stalled** — with buyers and sellers adjusting expectations.
