The Basics

What Is a Physician Loan?

A physician mortgage loan — sometimes called a "doctor loan" — is a specialized home financing program offered by select lenders. It was created to address the financial reality that many high-earning medical professionals face early in their careers:

  • High student loan debt that skews standard debt-to-income calculations
  • Little savings for a down payment after years of training
  • A future income that far outpaces their current resident or fellow salary
  • A strong professional track record that traditional underwriting doesn't capture

Physician loans are designed to look past these short-term challenges and recognize the full picture of who you are as a borrower.

Why It's Different

Key Benefits Worth Understanding

🏠

Low or No Down Payment

Many physician loan programs allow 0–5% down on loans up to $1M or more, without requiring private mortgage insurance (PMI).

📋

Student Loan Flexibility

Lenders may use a reduced percentage of your student loan balance — or even $0 if in deferment — when calculating your debt-to-income ratio.

📝

Employment Contract Accepted

You don't have to wait until your first paycheck. A signed offer or employment contract is often enough to qualify before you start.

📈

Higher Loan Limits

Physician loans frequently exceed conforming loan limits, making them suitable for higher-cost markets where many doctors practice.

⏱️

Timing That Works For You

Whether you're finishing residency, starting a new position, or transitioning to private practice — these loans are designed around your career arc.

🔍

Full Underwriting, Not Shortcuts

These are real mortgage loans — fully underwritten with standard protections. The flexibility is in how your profile is evaluated, not in cutting corners.

Common Questions

Things Doctors Usually Ask

I have $300K+ in student loans. Can I really qualify?

Yes — this is one of the most important ways physician loans differ. Traditional loans count your full monthly student loan payment against your debt-to-income ratio. Many physician loan programs use a much smaller figure, or exclude deferred loans entirely, making qualification far more realistic for new attendings and residents.

Is this just for residents and fellows, or can attendings use it too?

Both. Physician loans are available to residents, fellows, and fully practicing attendings. Some programs do have income or years-in-practice requirements, so eligibility varies — but this product is not limited to early-career physicians.

What's the catch? Why don't more people know about this?

There's no hidden catch, but there are tradeoffs worth understanding. Rates may be slightly higher than conventional loans, and not every lender offers them. The bigger reason most doctors don't know? These programs aren't heavily advertised — most people only find out through word of mouth or a knowledgeable loan officer.

Do I need perfect credit to qualify?

Not perfect, but strong credit does help. Most programs look for a 700+ credit score, though some lenders will work with scores in the mid-600s depending on other factors. If your score needs work, that's worth addressing before applying.

Can I use this loan to buy a second home or investment property?

Generally, no. Physician loans are structured for primary residences only. They're not intended for vacation properties or rental investments. Lenders typically verify this during the application process.

How is this different from an FHA loan?

FHA loans are government-backed and designed for buyers with lower credit scores or smaller down payments — but they require PMI (mortgage insurance premium) for the life of the loan in many cases and have lower loan limits. Physician loans are conventional products with no PMI requirement and much higher limits, built for a very different borrower profile.

I'm a pharmacist / dentist — does this apply to me?

Often yes. While the name says "physician," many lenders extend these programs to PharmDs, DDSs, DMDs, and other doctoral-level healthcare professionals. Eligibility varies by lender — it's one of the key questions to ask when exploring options.
